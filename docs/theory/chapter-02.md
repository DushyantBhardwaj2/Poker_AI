# Chapter Two: Expectation and Hourly Rate

## 🧠 Concept Overview
Mathematical expectation is the foundation of all gambling, including poker. It defines the amount a bet will average winning or losing over time. In poker, every decision—whether to fold, call, or raise—should be evaluated based on its mathematical expectation. The sum of all your expectations forms your hourly rate. Professional players focus exclusively on making plays with a positive expectation (+EV) and avoiding those with a negative expectation (-EV), trusting that the mathematics will manifest as profit in the long run.

## 🎯 Key Ideas
- **Mathematical Expectation (+EV / -EV):** The average amount you stand to win or lose per bet. You have the "best of it" when you have a positive expectation (the odds are in your favor) and the "worst of it" with a negative expectation.
- **Hourly Rate:** The amount of money you expect to win per hour, calculated by summing the expectations of your plays.
- **Results are Irrelevant in the Short Term:** Whether a specific +EV play wins or loses a given pot does not change its mathematical correctness.
- **Relative Expectation:** The "best" play is the one that maximizes your positive expectation or minimizes your negative expectation. Sometimes calling is better than raising if raising forces out opponents who would otherwise contribute more to the pot.
- **Saving Money is Making Money:** Folding a hand that has negative expectation directly adds to your hourly rate by minimizing losses.

## 📊 Detailed Explanation
Expectation is calculated by weighing the probability of winning against the pot odds. If someone offers you 2-to-1 odds on a coin flip (which is naturally 1-to-1), you have a positive expectation. Even if you lose the first ten flips, over 100 or 1,000 flips, the math guarantees a profit. 

In poker, this concept dictates your play. When the pot is offering you odds that are better than your mathematical chances of hitting your hand, making the call is +EV. If your chances of hitting are worse than the pot odds, calling is -EV.

Furthermore, analyzing expectation applies to more complex decisions, like whether to call or raise. If you have a very strong hand on the final betting round (the "river") and a player ahead of you bets, raising seems like the obvious choice. However, if your raise will cause the two players acting behind you to fold, while a simple call would keep them in the pot, the call might yield a higher positive expectation (two extra bets won instead of one).

Your hourly rate comes from the difference between how you play a hand optimally and how your opponents play incorrectly. The total amount of money your opponents cost themselves with incorrect play (minus the rake) becomes your win rate.

## ♠️ Case Studies

### Case Study 1: The Pot Odds Call
**Situation:** You are playing Limit Hold'em. On the turn, the pot is $100. Your opponent bets $20. You have a flush draw. You know there are 46 unseen cards, and 9 of them give you a flush (roughly a 4-to-1 underdog).
**Decision:** Call.
**Explanation:** You are getting $120 to $20, which is 6-to-1 pot odds. Since your odds of hitting the flush are 4-to-1, you have a positive expectation. In the long run, making this call earns you money, regardless of whether the flush hits on this specific hand.

### Case Study 2: The Multi-way Trap (Maximizing Expectation)
**Situation:** Playing Seven-Card Stud, you hit a hidden full house on the final card. The player to your right, who you suspect has two pair, bets $10. There are two players behind you who are loose and likely to call a single bet, but not a raise.
**Decision:** Call.
**Explanation:** If you raise, the loose players behind you will fold, and the original bettor might just call your raise (winning you $20 total from him). If you simply call, the loose players are highly likely to call the $10 as well. You win $10 from three different players ($30 total). Calling has a higher positive expectation than raising.

### Case Study 3: The Short Bankroll Exception
**Situation:** You are playing a No-Limit game. You have a marginal positive expectation (+EV) edge in a massive all-in pot (e.g., you are a 55% favorite for your entire bankroll). 
**Decision:** Fold (or avoid the situation).
**Explanation:** While the play is technically +EV, pushing small edges is incorrect if you are on a short bankroll. A loss here would bust you, preventing you from continuing to play and earning your hourly rate. It is sometimes correct to pass on a marginally profitable situation to guarantee you stay in the game to exploit much larger edges later.

## ⚠️ Common Mistakes
- **Being Results-Oriented:** Getting angry when a "bad beat" happens, rather than being happy that your opponent made a -EV call that earns you money in the long run.
- **Ignoring the Rake:** Failing to factor in the casino or house rake when calculating hourly rate or evaluating if a game is beatable.
- **Raising Automatically:** Raising with a strong hand without considering if a flat call would trap more players and yield a higher expected value.

## 💡 Pro Tips
- **The Stoicism of Math:** Understanding expectation gives you equanimity. When you fold correctly, you know you saved money. When you get outdrawn after getting your money in good, you know you earned money in theory. This mindset cures "tilt."
- **Observe Opponent Leaks:** Your hourly rate is directly tied to the specific mistakes your opponents make. Identify the players making the most -EV plays (e.g., calling too much) and sit near them.
